Goofari

20 Feb 2012

I’ve been writing about Google’s Safari privacy gaffe over the past few days, and there’s an interesting counter argument developing. If Apple didn’t have such stringent privacy defaults, the thinking goes, Google wouldn’t have had to exploit a loophole in Safari’s code. Unlike other browsers, Apple’s default privacy settings prohibit third-party cookies from being set without explicit user interaction. And, as we all know, most people don’t bother to change their default settings. So Apple’s actions must be an example of anti-competitive behavior or an attempt to lock down its customers.

John Battelle writes:

“But let’s step back a second here and ask: why do you think Apple has made it impossible for advertising-driven companies like Google to execute what are industry standard practices on the open web (dropping cookies and tracking behavior so as to provide relevant services and advertising)? Do you think it’s because Apple cares deeply about your privacy?

Really?

Or perhaps it’s because Apple considers anyone using iOS, even if they’re browsing the web, as “Apple’s customer,” and wants to throttle potential competitors…Might it be possible that Apple is using data as its weapon, dressed up in the PR friendly clothing of ”privacy protection” for users?

That’s at least a credible idea, I’d argue.”

There are a lot of things Apple does that give me pause, but I just can’t buy the idea that Apple is trying to screw over web companies with its default settings in Safari. It seems credible because of the way Apple has locked down (and continues to lock down) the app market for iOS and OS X. But since when is erring on the side of more online privacy a bad thing?
