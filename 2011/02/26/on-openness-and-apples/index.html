On Openness

26 Feb 2011

The other day I looked down at my desk and this is what I saw:

hmmmm…

Becoming an all-Apple user over the past three years wasn’t something I had concerned myself with before. But I took a hard look at my habits after Apple’s recently announced in-app subscription scheme and the new Mac App Store. These are just the latest moves in what appears to be Apple’s attempt to assert more control over the computers you own and the content you access.

Sure, for many of us Apple’s new plans are great. All you have to do is click a button, enter your Apple account password and you’re signed up for your favorite service or you’ve got a great new app for your Mac.

But with all this convenience comes a disturbing lack of control. Once you buy a piece of software from the Mac App Store, for example, it appears you are forever stuck with Apple as the middle man between you and your software maker. Whenever you need a software update, you have to wait for Apple to approve it. Worse, should something happen to cause your software maker to stop dealing with the Mac App Store or iTunes, you may be left out in the cold.

The same is true for Apple’s new in-app subscription policies, which have the potential to negatively affect service providers such as Rhapsody or Netflix (if they fall under the new policy). Rhapsody has already said the subscription scheme would be financially untenable for the company. The music subscription provider has also issued some veiled threats that it might sue Apple over in-app subscriptions. So far Netflix isn’t talking and who knows what kind of effect in-app subscriptions will have on Amazon.

I find it troubling that Apple wants to dictate terms to me, the services I use and the software I want to buy. The worst part is Apple’s doing this with some of the best hardware and online retail infrastructure I’ve ever used. There’s no getting around the fact that iOS devices and Mac computers provide a great user experience. I am far more productive on a Mac than I ever was on a Windows machine. My computer hardly ever crashes, I almost never have to run a resource-hogging anti-virus program (although that could change) and getting around the system to open a new app, new browser tab or whatever is far more efficient on a Mac–at least for me it is. Then there’s all those great apps for iPad and iPhone that trap my content and data in little silos that can’t interact with each other. And let’s not forget how easy it is to buy DRM-laden movies from iTunes or e-books for Amazon’s Kindle app.

As much as I love my Apple machines and services, I also love the idea of open standards and open source software. There are lots of reasons to love openness: it’s cheaper (often free), hackable (meaning you can do anything you want to it), and is relatively independent from corporate interests (but not always).

I often toy with the idea of switching over to Linux and using as much open source software as I can to replace everything I do now.

But then I sit here looking at all these beautiful Apple devices and all those great apps that divvy up the Web into little chunks (but are so much easier to use), and I know how hard it would be for me to give them up.

That’s when I realized the greatest threat to openness isn’t Apple, Microsoft, Google or even Comcast …it’s me.

If you want to hear an interesting discussion about this problem check out Jesse Brown interviewing Tim Wu on the Search Engine podcast.
