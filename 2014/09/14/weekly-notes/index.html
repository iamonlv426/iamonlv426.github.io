Weekly Notes

14 Sep 2014

Working as a tech journalist is such a weird job. Most of the time what we do is utterly unimportant. We report on the latest gadget, some cool website or app, or explain how to more effectively use Google.

But since the Internet and our devices are increasingly crucial elements of daily life, there are those times when what we report on is absolutely essential.

This week those two contradictory roles came into stark contrast.

Two big stories were in the headlines this week: Apple's debut of the iPhone 6 and Apple Watch, and the looming end of the public comment period for the FCC's so-called "Open Internet guidelines."

For some the debut of the iPhone is an absolutely pivotal moment--why else would otherwise sane people get up at 3 a.m. just to be the first in line to buy a phone? But in the grand scheme of things another iPhone is just another iPhone, only better.

That's not the case with the FCC's guidelines that may soon become public policy. Under the new guidelines, major ISPs would be allowed to charge Internet companies for faster delivery of their traffic. This policy would inevitably determine how quickly or slowly you can connect to a given website regardless of the Internet speeds you're paying for.

Like the SOPA/PIPA protests before it, the FCC's new rules have the potential to do serious harm to the open Internet we have now. At least if you're in the U.S. Although U.S. policy on these matters has a funny way of influencing the path other countries take.

Whether you're for or against the FCC's proposal, reporting on the issue to bring it to public attention is important as the new rules could drastically effect how you get online. And how you get online determines how effectively you can do your job, access information to improve your professional skills, study at university, stay informed with the news sources of your choice, pay your bills, do your banking, speak with long distance friends and relatives, and speak out on politics and public policy.

How the FCC is about to treat the Internet is no small matter.

As for the iPhone 6, that is one beautiful phone. I want one.
